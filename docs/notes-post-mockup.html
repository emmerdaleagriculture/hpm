When to top a paddock — and when to leave it

Topping March 2026 · 6 min read

Topping is one of those jobs that looks straightforward and isn't. Cut at the right moment and the field rewards you for the rest of the season. Cut at the wrong one and you're undoing growth, encouraging weeds, or just making a mess. After twenty years of getting it right and wrong, here's what I actually look for.

The short answer

Top when grass has set seed but before the field has gone over completely — usually mid-to-late June for a first cut, with a second cut in late August or early September if needed. Don't top when the ground is sodden. Don't top in a heatwave. Don't top below about three inches.

That covers maybe 80% of cases. The other 20% is where the judgement comes in.

What you're actually trying to do

Topping serves three purposes, and which one is most important depends on the field:

  • Removing rough, ungrazed patches — horses are picky grazers and leave dock, thistle, and the long ryegrass tussocks they don't fancy. Topping evens the field out.
  • Knocking back weeds before they seed — ragwort, docks, thistles. Cut before the seed heads fully form and you reduce next year's problem dramatically.
  • Encouraging tillering — grass plants respond to being cut by sending out more shoots from the base, which thickens the sward over time.
"The mistake I see most often is topping for tidiness. A neat-looking field isn't the same as a healthy one."

When to leave it alone

There are days when the right job is to drive past and come back next week. The most common are:

When the ground is wet. A loaded tractor on wet ground compacts the soil and ruts the surface. Both take longer to recover than the topping was worth. If you can see daylight in your tyre print, leave it.

In high summer drought. Topping in 28°C and no rain for a fortnight stresses the grass plants and exposes the crown to the sun. They take a beating they didn't need.

When the field's already short. If your horses have grazed it down, topping below three inches removes the leaf the plant needs to recover.

Frequency

Most paddocks need one or two cuts a year. More than that and you're either over-managing or there's something else going on (probably a grazing pattern issue or a weed problem that wants treating directly).

The exception is a paddock you're trying to renovate — somewhere with serious weed pressure or a ley that's losing density. Three or four cuts in a season can knock back the weed seed bank and let the grass recover.
